Overview
What we’re building with ShiftLync
A focused MVP to prove that we can reduce scheduling chaos, close shift vacancies faster, and make relief staffing more transparent for Canadian pharmacies – starting in small remote cities.
MVP budget target: ~ $6,000

Single source of truth for pharmacy shifts.

Today, scheduling lives in Excel sheets, WhatsApp groups, email chains, and agency portals. ShiftLync centralizes internal schedules, vacancies, staff sharing, and relief pharmacists in one system – with a realistic MVP we can ship quickly and iterate on.

Primary adopters: independent & remote pharmacies
Initial cities: Thunder Bay, ON · Cold Lake, AB
Focus: scheduling + relief shift lifecycle
Goal of this page: align on which MVP scenario (A or B) we start with, how it fits into a $6k budget, and what technical scope is required for a successful, realistic launch.
Core problem
Scheduling chaos
Gaps, double-booking, no visibility across locations, heavy manual work by managers and relief companies.
Target outcome
Time ↓ 50–60%
Reduce manager time spent on scheduling, coordination, and chasing relief pharmacists for coverage.
Competitive edge
No 20–30% cut
Direct matching instead of high agency markup, while still enabling structured, auditable workflows.
MVP scope level
Balanced
Strong enough to be useful, lean enough to ship within budget and time with a small dev team.
Context
Market reality we are designing for
The MVP should reflect how pharmacies actually operate: long hours, staff shortages, last-minute sick calls, and growing dependence on relief pharmacists and staffing companies.
Design for real Canadian workflows, not idealized ones.
Key pain points (pharmacy side)
Problem set
No unified schedule: Excel + paper + messaging apps = mistakes and no single truth.
Shift vacancies are reactive: recognized late, broadcast manually, filled slowly.
Agencies are expensive: 20–30% cut on pharmacist pay, yet still manual coordination.
Compliance & risk: double-booking, fatigue, missing required certifications or methadone training.
Remote cities hit hardest
Limited pool of trusted relief pharmacists
Key pain points (pharmacist side)
No central view of opportunities: they juggle texts, groups, and calls.
Unclear expectations: every pharmacy has different workflow, software, and rules.
Delayed payment & invoicing: bookkeeping is manual and annoying.
What MVP must prove
One system is enough: managers should feel safe moving off Excel/WhatsApp for scheduling.
Relief shifts fill faster: measurable decrease in time-to-fill for common scenarios.
Low friction onboarding: small teams in remote cities can adopt without IT support.
Design constraint: the MVP must fit into a lean budget (~$6k) while still touching all parts of the shift lifecycle at a basic but usable level.
Decision point
Two MVP scenarios to choose from
Both scenarios assume the same budget envelope (~$6k), but trade off depth vs breadth. Scenario A is safer and quicker; Scenario B is more ambitious and closer to the full vision.
Goal: pick A or B as our first dev target.
Scenario A – Focused Internal Scheduling MVP
Lean · Low complexity
Scope: Build a strong internal scheduling tool for 1–3 locations, then layer external relief later.
Included in Scenario A Notes
Internal schedule management (pharmacists, techs, assistants) Single source of truth for each pharmacy/group.
Availability & time-off requests Staff manage their own availability; managers approve.
Shift vacancy detection Automatic identification of uncovered shifts.
Internal posting & pickup Staff within the organization can offer to cover gaps.
Basic tags & roles (e.g., methadone, IV, Kroll) Start simple, avoid complex rule engine.
Basic reporting (coverage, hours) Exportable CSV/Excel only.
Pros: faster build, smaller attack surface, easier QA, good for first pilot in remote city.
Cons: does not fully differentiate from generic scheduling tools yet; relief value is postponed.
Recommended if we want fastest path to pilot Tech stack can be simpler
Scenario B – Scheduling + Staff Sharing + Relief
Balanced · Higher impact
Scope: Everything in Scenario A plus an initial “mini marketplace” for relief and friend-pharmacy sharing.
Additional in Scenario B Notes
Friend-pharmacy network (staff sharing) Selected pharmacies can share shifts and staff within a trusted circle.
Relief pharmacist profiles & simple onboarding Name, license, certifications, preferred areas, hourly rate.
Open shift listing for relief pharmacists Relief can see relevant open shifts and request to work.
Basic matching rules Match by role, location radius, availability, tags (e.g., methadone, software).
Simple acceptance flow Manager approves one candidate; shift becomes locked.
Very basic invoicing placeholder Downloadable shift summary that can be used for invoicing (no full accounting yet).
Pros: closer to final vision, immediately useful for both pharmacies and relief, more defensible product.
Cons: more moving parts, higher complexity for a $6k budget, must be carefully scoped.
Recommended if we can strictly control scope & reuse components Needs clear non-goals
Non-goals for MVP (both scenarios)
Important for scope control
No full payroll or CRA-level accounting No complex union/collective agreement rules engine No advanced AI scheduling optimization in MVP No mobile apps in phase 1 (web-first)
MVP should be a clean, reliable web app that proves workflow value. AI, mobile apps, advanced automation and deep accounting integrations can come in later phases once core adoption is validated.
Technical
High-level system & data model (for MVP)
Enough structure for a scalable product, but simple enough for a small team to implement within budget. The core idea: organizations, locations, users, roles, and shifts linked in a clean schema.
Design once, usable for both scenarios A & B.
Core entities
Organization / Group: corporate entity (owner or relief company).
Location / Pharmacy: physical site, linked to an organization.
User: pharmacist, technician, assistant, manager, admin.
Role assignment: user + role (e.g., staff pharmacist, relief pharmacist, manager).
Shift: location + time + role + tags (methadone, IV, Kroll, etc.).
Availability: user availability blocks and time-off requests.
Application / booking: link between user and shift (offer to work, approval status).
Must support both staff and relief pharmacists Audit-friendly (who changed what, when)
Key flows to design
Internal scheduling flow: manager creates schedule template → assigns shifts → staff view & confirm.
Vacancy lifecycle: gap detected → posted as open shift → internal staff / relief request → manager confirms.
Friend-pharmacy sharing (Scenario B): open shift shared to trusted partners only.
Architecture level for MVP
Web app first: responsive web (desktop + mobile browser) – no native apps in MVP.
Single region deploy: Canadian data residency if possible, or clear plan.
Admin tools: light internal admin surface to manage pilot users and locations.
Ask for CTO: recommend tech stack (frontend, backend, DB) that balances speed, scalability, and our ability to iterate quickly within budget.
Execution
Phased roadmap around MVP
Each phase should be small enough to ship, learn, and adjust. MVP is Phase 1; later phases deepen automation and monetization.
We keep phases small, learn from pilots, and adjust scope.
Phase 1 – MVP Launch
Internal scheduling + (optional) basic relief
Implement Scenario A or B core features.
Onboard 2–4 pharmacies in Thunder Bay / Cold Lake.
Measure time-to-fill, scheduling time, and user satisfaction.
Phase 2 – Automation & Insights
Smarter matching & rules
Introduce smarter shift suggestions based on patterns.
Add basic conflict checks (fatigue, overlapping jobs).
Better dashboards for coverage, costs, and usage.
Phase 3 – Monetization & Scale
Pricing & integrations
Introduce subscription tiers and relief transaction fees.
Add accounting/payroll integrations for larger groups.
Phase 4 – Advanced & AI
Optimization & training
AI-assisted schedule suggestions and risk alerts.
Training modules, certifications, and compliance tracking.
Commercial
Business model aligned with MVP
Early stages focus on adoption and value. Monetization should be simple and aligned with how pharmacies and relief pharmacists already think about costs.
We start simple, then add complexity later.
For pharmacies (groups & independents)
Phase 1: free pilot for early adopters; we collect data and testimonials.
Phase 2: simple SaaS pricing (e.g., per location per month) once value is proven.
Optional relief fee: small platform fee per successfully filled relief shift (well below 20–30% agency cut).
Key promise: lower friction and cost than agencies.
For relief pharmacists
Free to join: no monthly fee initially, focus on adoption and liquidity.
Optional upgrades later: advanced reporting, bookkeeping, or premium visibility for heavy users.
MVP does not need full billing or payouts; a simple “shift summary” that can be used as invoice backup is enough to start conversations and test willingness to pay.
